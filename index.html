Book Review: The Rosie Project

This book came with a commanding recommendation from a good friend, "best book ever". I hadn't read a novel in more than 5 years, and to break that streak with a love story sounded abominable. To top this, I discovered that Bill Gates had included it in his 2014 summer reading list. I therefore had a high bar for the book when I decided to take the plunge.

I started reading this book at midgnight. 4 hours later, my bed time read had turned into the book-that-kept-me-awake-way-past-mignight read. The plot of "The Rosie Project" is gripping, funny and embedded with nerdy facts. I smiled and laughed quite a bit. It's been a while since I had read a novel with magic powers to uplift your mood.

The protagonist Don Tillman, is an unmarried genetics professor with a penchant for rationality, organization and science. He finds comfort and mind peace in routine, planning and habits. Some highlights from the book which are worth implementing in our lives:

- Cooking meals and the adoption of the "Standardized Meal System". One of the benefits being "Excellent food, superior to most restaurants at a much lower price"
- Scheduling meetings and deliberate planning of activites on calendar. He asks a very pertinent question: "Why do people value othersâ€™ time so little?

My friends who have read this book were of the opinion that Don Tillman is the Sheldon Cooper (from the popular TV series The Big Bang Theory) of Genetics from Australia. I however felt that Professor Don Tillman is a realistic and more likeable character in comparison to Sheldon Cooper. Chief among these differences being:
- Independence: Don Tillman doesn't depend on another human being to protect him/drive him around.
- Maturity: Don Tillman doesn't whine or project weakness.
If you are looking for an escape for a few hours (It's a short read at 290 pages), look no further than the Rosie Project by Graeme Simison.

Prompt: CANNED FILM.

A teenage love story goes hay-wire when Sharon discovers that she no longer has citizenship rights to the country she had inhibited for the past 20 years. Sharon in a precipitous act decides to become an astronaut and volunteers to become a permanent citizen of the international space station. The solitude in the space station in the company of far-away stars, asteroids and comets makes her realise she missed home. She decides to create a home of her own, procreating with donor sperms from male colleagues and colonizes ISS.
Years later, her daughter falls in love with a visiting astronaut and wants to defect to planet earth. Sharon denies permission for her daughter to move.
